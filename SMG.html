I. Executive Summary

Overview of Portfolio Performance

From early-September to mid-December,  JW Investments’ initial $100,000 quick growth portfolio went up in net value by $38,355.80, beating the S&P 500 by 33.22%. $40,000 was initially invested into longing various Stalwart stocks and $10,000  in a short position on Shell. The portfolio suffered a loss in October but recovered after gold ETFs rocketed in value. Securities purchased in early November revolved around anticipation towards quarterly earnings reports, including  Apple, Alibaba, and Rogers. After the controversy in the pharmaceutical sector revolving around Martin Shkreli took place in November, we researched and decided to short sell Valeant and Kalobios in December.

Research Methods

Investment of the quick growth portfolio was based on two steps: company background information through fundamental analysis, and technical indicators.  We followed the theory of buying into companies that we were familiar with so we could predict and understand past and present trends.  We researched those companies and created a shortlist based on expected trends based on future company plans and technical indicators for the correct buying window. In addition, we performed a similar process for companies that would be impacted from major events.

Investment Objectives and Strategy

Our primary objectives for our quick growth portfolio were to achieve 15% growth over a three month period while minimizing risk and maintaining liquidity.

The strategy we employed to meet these objectives was to diversify our portfolio:

 i) Internationally, to expose ourselves to a variety of markets with high growth potential.

ii) Across sectors, to minimize the risk of one sector crashing.

Strategy Success and Future Improvements

JW Investments’ 38.36% return from the portfolio reflects the astounding success of our investments and strategies. Challenges were overcome through diversification and knowledgeability in our securities. Companies that were chosen through our investing theory of investing into familiar companies were our highest profiting, because they were companies that we had the background and basis to know the optimal time to buy and sell, and to be able to base those decisions on more than ticker trends. Areas that JW Investments plan on changing for the future include limiting investments in unknown and unpredictable territory,  decreasing the number of day-trades, and  a firmer stance on our portfolio securities.

II. Analysis of Portfolio Performance

In a span of 3.5 months, from September to December, JW Investments’ quick growth portfolio increased in value by $38,500 on an initial $100,000. A return on investment 33.22% over the S&P 500. Our decisions were based on in-depth fundamental analysis, research into major events, earnings reports, jobs reports, and personal knowledge of favourable companies. Our investment ideology is further expanded upon in section III. Rationale.
        We invested approximately $40,000 in long and $10,000 in short stock positions, in blue chip companies. All were companies we knew and bought based on known performance, economic factors, and events within the companies. By early November, quarterly earnings reports were to be released, and expectations towards those impacted a majority of our decisions. These positions produced an average return of 6% with minimal risk, given the size and strength of these companies. Our portfolio experienced only one major loss (RDSA) with all other positions breaking even or netting a profit. However, this loss was only 2% of our entire portfolio and is attributed to an unexpected rally in the price while oil prices were dropping and expansion plans were cancelled. The remaining $50,000 was placed into ETFs and other stock positions. We chose to take on a high volatility position in gold miner ETFs with confidence in positive returns. With China and U.S. in economic slowdowns and jobs reports barely meeting the already low expectations gold prices were being driven up. Gold proceeded to surge to a 3 month high leaving us with a net profit of $10,000. Exorbitantly high drug prices and the controversy they brought to the pharmaceutical industry lead to our eventual short position for Valeant and KaloBios, which both subsequently dropped heavily in value creating $24,000 in unrealized gains. Overall, our approach of fundamental analysis and research, while taking technical indicators into account paid off greatly. The quick growth portfolio exceeded its objective of generating a 15% return, and successfully minimized risk and maintained liquidity.

III. Rationale

  1. Research Conducted Prior to Purchasing Stocks

        The investment of our portfolio was primarily based on the background conducted (with fundamental and qualitative analysis) of the company, and secondarily based on technical aspects of the stock performance. Companies were shortlisted as candidates first through a fundamental analysis of companies that we were familiar with. After a company made the shortlist, we then narrowed down purchases using Yahoo Finance for technical indicators like the MACD and Moving Average.

            Fundamental analysis on familiar companies was the key theme behind many of our stock choices. The primary benefit of this strategy is that it allowed us to see company performance outside of simply tickers and stocks, and gave us the knowledge to act based on company history and expectations. Additionally, this allowed for us to assess continued performance based on product/service, and judge the correct time to sell. Apple was one example of this. We first conducted a qualitative analysis by visiting several Apple stores to survey customer volume with our own eyes when the iPad Pro was released on November 11. Next, we read more into iPhone 6s reviews and only found positive ones. With a positive qualitative analysis, we began a detailed historical analysis. We noticed that earnings reports have consistently delivered expected high reports almost every year, and with the success of Apple’s new products, this year should be no exception. We decided to buy shares based on high anticipation for the soon to be released quarterly earnings reports.  We moved on to conduct a technical analysis to analyze market health. MACD indicated a divergence from the signal, pointing to an end to the bearish trends. Both SMA and EMA showed that the stock was undervalued. MFI indicated 48, meaning that the upward potential is reliable. Additionally, a moderately high BETA of 1.44 and wide Bollinger Bands show high volatility, giving room for our predicted growth. Several other stocks such as Alibaba, Rogers, and Ford were also chosen based on similar rationale, and similarly bullish technical indicators.    

Another key factor that impacted our research was major events going on in the world that would influence the performance of companies and ETFs. Major events have a large influence on the healthiness of many stocks; it was essential to keep up with news so we could be knowledgeable in the state of the industries. For example, a major event is what lead us to short KaloBios. Since September 2015, Martin Shkreli has been surrounded by controversy and widespread criticism as a result of raising the price of an antiparasitic drug 5,556%. Researching further into pharmaceuticals, We realised KaloBios was far overvalued after a large stake of Kalobios was acquired by Shkreli’s investor group, and Shkreli was named CEO. Technical indicators such as wide Bollinger Bands and high beta (3.3) all point to high volatility. The MACD experienced a dramatic rise, and the EMA and SMA displaying far lower values than the actual price point to KaloBios being overvalued. Additionally, Parabolic Sars, RCI, and ROC indicators all point to a bearish trend. Based on those reasons, history and tech indicators, we decided to short sell. After Shkreli’s conveniently timed securities fraud arrest, Kalobios’ stock dropped and was removed from the market.

Shell was a company that was short sold based on both fundamental analysis and a major event. The oil index had been bearish in September due to action in the Middle-East, with downwards trends all across. We decided to familiarize ourselves further with oil companies before acquiring securities in the sector. On September 28, Shell announced that it planned to cancel plans of expanding oil drilling to Alaska, due to a lack of oil found. With expectations for Shell’s stock value to drop further, we conducted more research about company history on sites such as Bloomberg and Motley Fool, and concluded Shell was a possible short sell candidate. Technical indicators all pointed to a bearish week: Parabolic Sars indicated a sell, MACD crossed below the signal, and RSI indicated a 25 (high overselling).

  1. Strategy Used to Diversify Portfolio

During a small time span, we had to produce large returns, meaning some risk was necessary. To minimize our risk, we invested in companies from many sectors. This resulted in overall gains much larger than any losses experienced in any one sector. We developed and maintained an exceptionally diverse portfolio, exposing ourselves to different countries and different types of stocks. We held bonds, mutual funds throughout the simulation for added stability. By purchasing positions in a few volatile small-cap stocks in a bullish market we increased our prospective growth. We did this while purchasing a few large to mega cap stalwart stocks just before they released quarterly earnings. Our primary objective through diversification was to hedge our portfolio while exposing ourselves to large growth potentials.

A focus was placed on holding many forms of securities such as Fast Growers, Blue-Chip, Cyclical, and Asset Plays. Our fast grower positions made it possible to produce larger returns in the limited time available. They came with a high level of risk. Holding Stalwart stocks provided stability in the case of economic swings, as a result of the company's offerings and steady growth. Cyclical stocks generated more predictable growth based off of market factors and earning reports. The ETFs purchased are at higher risk and their success was dependent on our observations that included currency values linking to the economy. The short amount of time allotted for trading led us to place all but the mandatory security in stocks and leveraged ETFs. We chose this rather than bonds and mutual funds since they tend to produce lower returns over short periods of time.

   

Diversifying our portfolio internationally was also a major component of our strategy.  In order to expose our portfolio to rapidly developing markets and other stable ones around the world, we invested in companies based in different markets. This both hedged us against the risk of one market failing and more importantly opened our opportunities for growth.

We formulated a simple set of values and questions to check stocks against. Using these questions we decided if a stock was a viable option to long or short without exposing ourselves to too much unknown risk.  The categories for performance, value (under, over) influences and how the stock compares were the basis of our fundamental and qualitative analysis:

1. Performance

  • Historical: Does the company have a healthy history?
  • Operational: What is the potential for growth?

2. Value

  • Current: Is the stock reflective of the company’s value?
  • Intrinsic: Should the stock price be above or below its current location?

3. Influences

  • Volatility: How easily influenced is the stock/ how volatile is the stock?
  • Future: What factors have the potential to impact the future price?

4. Comparison

  • Industry: How is the company performing versus others in its industry?
  • Economic: How is the company performing relative to the market?

  1. How Investments Fit into Strategy

The stocks that we invested in provided reasonable volatility while not exposing us to undue risk. We diversified our portfolio by investing internationally and in many sectors. Shell headquartered in Holland, Alibaba headquartered in China, and  Toyota Motors headquartered in Japan, along with the main base of American companies demonstrate a well geographically diversified portfolio.  

We found many companies to invest in through our checklist. Some companies that were healthy with high growth potential and performing above the industry and market average were Alibaba, Apple, gold miners, and oppositely pharmaceuticals. We chose to long the first set of stocks just before influential events such as earning reports for apple and economic reports from the Chinese government and US jobs reports. We chose to short pharmaceuticals, specifically  based on strong negative press and underperformance combined with greatly overvalued stocks.

  1. Proposed Changes in Strategy for Future Investments

Though our overall portfolio was an astounding success, there are many areas we will improve on in future investments.
        First, we learned that we should draw back on investing  large amounts of capital into unknown, and therefore unpredictable territory. Shell is the major offender of this issue. At the surface, it had seemed like the perfect time to short sell: Tumbling oil prices, and an Alaskan expansion that was just cancelled due to lack of oil. However, more patience in our position and we would have concluded that investors only saw it as a minor tumble. We learned that investing into lesser knowledgeable areas should be done with both more patience and more knowledge.
   Less day-to-day trading is something we would change in the future. We accumulated a $8,899.64 cost in commission; almost 25% of our total return. With the 1% commission rate per trade, our holdings had to earn at least 2% to break even. Profits from day-to-day trades were severely diminished as of a result. Additionally, with the inability to act instantly like automated portfolios, any day-to-day trades would be based on what would already be considered old news, limiting us to act on only residual shifts past the initial influxes. With high commission, holding positions for longer durations is more profitable.
   Finally, we plan on showing more sturdiness with our decisions.  Many early drops in performance of holdings, especially the safe steady growers would have recovered their value, even rose further had we held on for longer. Human emotions resulted in us acting against the logical facts. Some long positions were sold on bearish weeks where temporary drops in performance should have been expected. In the future, we plan to hold onto less risky stocks for longer durations during weak weeks to give time for recovery (under the assumption that no major events will swing the decision), and not let noise affect decisions.
